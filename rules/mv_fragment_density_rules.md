# MV Fragment Density Rules

Use these rules for MV, youth film, relationship fragments, lifestyle memory pieces, casual but beautiful footage, and any prompt where the user wants "氛围", "生活碎片", "随手拍但好看", "青春感", "梦感", "关系感", or "MV".

The key lesson from the case library: MV fragments are short, but they are not vague. Each fragment is a complete observable moment with a clear subject, object, body reaction, spatial relationship, and sensory trace.

## Fragment, Not Summary

Do not summarize a mood:

```text
她们在家里相处，气氛温柔，有生活感。
```

Write fragments:

```text
Ling 端着玻璃杯从画面左侧经过，Yingying 抱着小物从沙发边转身，两人在门框与茶几之间差点撞上；两人同时停住，身体各自向后收半步，视线短暂碰到又错开，玻璃杯边缘在阴天天光里闪一下，狭窄动线让这个家突然显得多了一个人。
```

## Required Fragment Jobs

Each MV fragment must do at least one of these jobs:

- **relationship evidence** — shows the relationship through distance, touch, refusal, help, shared object, or synchronized stillness
- **life trace** — shows the space being used or gently disturbed
- **memory object** — gives one prop enough identity to become memorable
- **attention transfer** — moves attention from foreground to face, hand to object, reflection to body, sound to reveal
- **aftertaste** — lands on a held image that feels like it could sit in an MV edit

Do not include a fragment whose only job is "cover the brief".

## Case-Library Patterns

Use these patterns as reusable directing grammar, not as fixed content:

### 1. Light Disruption

One character disrupts the other's stable routine with a small object or action.

Required details:

- stable routine before interruption
- interrupting object clearly visible
- first defensive reaction
- second softer reaction or unresolved pause
- anchor line preventing the object from drifting

Best for: hats, books, phones, snacks, blankets, headphones, room objects.

### 2. Used Space

The room tells the relationship by showing how it has been touched.

Required details:

- floor/table/bed/window/fish tank/doorway as spatial anchor
- small mess or object displacement
- one character causes it
- another character notices, passes, fixes, or ignores it
- sound of object, fabric, paper, water, or footstep

Best for: cohabitation, daily life, moving in, casual friendship, family-like scenes.

### 3. Partial Body / Partial Face

Do not always show a full face. Use hands, shoulders, knees, hair edge, reflection, or clothing corner to preserve identity while making composition less ordinary.

Required details:

- which body part is visible
- what identity anchor remains visible
- what is hidden and why
- foreground or reflection mechanism
- final attention landing point

Best for: youth MV, intimacy, awkwardness, dream fragments, phone calls, hiding emotion.

### 4. Reflection / Occlusion Memory

A shot becomes memorable because the subject is seen through another surface.

Required details:

- surface type: water, glass, fish tank, window, mirror, phone screen, table reflection, plastic, rain
- what the surface distorts or hides
- what remains clear
- how motion affects the reflection
- what sound belongs to the surface

Best for: dream, rain, room memory, loneliness, indirect emotion.

### 5. Third-Life Anchor

An animal, object, lamp, plant, or room detail acts like a third participant rather than decoration.

Required details:

- the third element has stable identity
- it changes or reacts slightly
- it creates a triangle, buffer, or attention path
- people relate through it rather than posing beside it
- the shot lands with it still participating

Best for: cats, pets, plush toys, fish tanks, plants, music players, food, products.

### 6. Aftertaste Still

The final shot does not explain. It shows the changed state after the action.

Required details:

- what changed from the beginning
- what remains still
- what tiny motion continues
- where the viewer's eye lands
- why the frame can loop or linger

Best for: endings, memory points, quiet emotional turns.

## MV Shot Density Checklist

Before output, check every MV/lifestyle fragment:

- Does the shot have a concrete situation, not just a pretty setup?
- Does something physical happen within the shot, even if tiny?
- Does another person, object, animal, surface, or space respond?
- Is the composition doing more than normal centered coverage?
- Does the shot include at least one sensory trace: fabric, footstep, breath, water, paper, glass, fur, bloom, shadow, reflection, or room tone?
- Could this shot already be cut into an MV timeline as a complete 1-3 second fragment?

If any answer is no, revise the shot before final output.

## What To Avoid

- Do not turn MV fragments into plot summaries.
- Do not make every shot a full-face beauty close-up.
- Do not write "生活感" without life traces.
- Do not write "关系感" without distance, touch, refusal, help, eye contact, shared object, or synchronized pause.
- Do not write "梦感" by adding particles. Use reflection, occlusion, scale, overexposure, unusual angle, sound distance, or incomplete body framing.
- Do not let casual footage become bland. Casual still needs selected detail.

## Density Target

For MV/lifestyle prompts, the case-library target is:

- 5-8 shots for a 15-second fragment when the goal is montage.
- 3-5 shots for a single concentrated interaction.
- Every shot should have one clear memory or relationship mechanism.
- At least one shot should avoid full-face direct presentation and use partial body, reflection, occlusion, low angle, high angle, foreground, or object-led framing.

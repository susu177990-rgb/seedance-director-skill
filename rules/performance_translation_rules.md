# Performance Translation Rules

Use these rules whenever a prompt includes a person, creature, character, mascot, robot, or object that must express emotion or personality through behavior.

## Core Requirement

Never leave emotion as an abstract label. Convert mood, personality, relationship, and inner change into visible control instructions.

For each important character or expressive subject, define:

- gaze target and gaze change
- mouth/jaw behavior
- breathing or pause rhythm
- hand action
- body weight and posture
- movement speed
- reaction timing
- concealment or leakage of emotion
- start state and end state

## Acting Control Layers

Use at least three layers for important emotional beats:

1. Face: eyes, eyelids, brows, mouth, jaw, cheek tension.
2. Hands: grip, release, fidget, hesitation, object contact, finger pressure.
3. Body: shoulder, neck, chest, spine, hip, weight shift, distance from others.
4. Breath/rhythm: held breath, shallow inhale, delayed exhale, action pause, recovery.
5. Gaze: avoidance, fixation, glance-return, looking past someone, looking at a prop instead of a person.

Choose the layers that are visible in the shot size. Do not describe tiny finger details in wide shots.

## Emotion To Behavior

### Suppressed Sadness

- eyes avoid direct contact and settle on a nearby object
- mouth tries to stay neutral but corners lose strength
- chin lowers before the face turns away
- hand keeps doing a practical action too carefully
- breath stops briefly before a controlled exhale

### Nervousness

- gaze checks the exit, other person's hands, or a noisy object
- thumb presses an edge, cup, sleeve, phone, or bag strap
- shoulders lift slightly and do not fully drop
- answer starts late or action pauses half a beat
- body leans away while face tries to stay polite

### Attraction / Curiosity

- gaze returns after pretending to look elsewhere
- hand reaches halfway then slows
- body angle opens by a small amount
- smile appears first in the eyes, then is suppressed or delayed
- distance closes through a small practical action

### Controlled Anger

- jaw tightens before the voice or movement changes
- hand becomes still instead of explosive
- gaze fixes on one point longer than normal
- shoulders stay square but breath becomes shallow
- object contact becomes too precise or too forceful

### Fear

- head turns before the body follows
- eyes search edges of the frame, doorway, reflection, or sound source
- hands protect the center of the body or freeze mid-action
- step becomes shorter and quieter
- breath is audible or visibly withheld

### Luxury Calm

- movement is slow, economical, and unhurried
- fingertips touch surfaces lightly and precisely
- face changes minimally
- gaze does not chase the camera
- body remains balanced, with no nervous corrections

### Loneliness

- body becomes smaller inside the frame
- gaze loses a clear target and rests on an empty space
- hands stop before finishing a normal action
- shoulders lower without dramatic collapse
- background sound continues while the character does not respond

### Defiance

- chin lifts slightly before the body commits
- gaze meets the target late but holds once it arrives
- hand releases support or lets an object drop out of dependency
- step moves into light, center, or open space
- breathing becomes steadier by the end

### Shame

- gaze drops before the other person's reaction is shown
- mouth opens as if to explain, then closes
- hand covers a small area rather than hiding the whole face
- body turns a few degrees away
- feet remain planted while the upper body wants to leave

### Exhaustion

- movement begins with intent but loses height or speed
- shoulders hang before the face admits fatigue
- hand uses furniture, wall, or prop for brief support
- blink is slower
- breath arrives after the movement rather than before

## Personality Through Action

Translate personality into repeated behavior, not labels:

- precise person: aligns objects, uses small controlled hand paths, stops movement cleanly
- impulsive person: moves before looking, changes direction mid-action, interrupts their own gesture
- guarded person: keeps object or furniture between self and others, answers with eyes before mouth
- confident person: enters the frame without checking the camera, uses fewer corrections, owns negative space
- fragile person: avoids hard contact, keeps hands close to body, reacts to sound before sight
- playful person: shifts weight, teases distance, turns ordinary props into small games

## Shot-Size Discipline

- Wide shot: use body direction, distance, path, silhouette, and relationship to space.
- Medium shot: use hands, shoulders, torso, gaze direction, and object contact.
- Close-up: use eyes, eyelids, mouth, jaw, breath, tiny head movement.
- Insert: use object pressure, grip change, pause, release, tremor, moisture, surface contact.

## Bad To Good

Weak:

```text
她很紧张。
```

Strong:

```text
她没有立刻抬头，右手拇指反复压住杯沿，眼神短暂扫向门口又收回，嘴角维持礼貌但下颌绷住。
```

Weak:

```text
他表现得很克制。
```

Strong:

```text
他先把话咽回去，手指从桌沿松开又重新按住，眼神停在对方肩侧而不是眼睛，呼吸慢半拍后才抬头。
```

Weak:

```text
她很高级很松弛。
```

Strong:

```text
她的动作没有多余修正，指尖轻触瓶身后立刻离开，肩颈保持展开，眼神不追随镜头，只在产品高光出现时轻微下移。
```

## Feasibility Guard

- Do not stack too many micro-actions into one second.
- Match the described detail to the shot size.
- One emotional beat should have one dominant visible signal and one supporting signal.
- Avoid facial expression chains that require many exact changes in a single shot.

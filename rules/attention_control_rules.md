# Attention Control Rules

Use these rules as the director's first principle: control what the viewer notices, in what order, and when the image lands.

## Core Requirement

Before finalizing shot details, silently define the segment attention path. Do not output it as a standalone module unless the user asks for planning. In the final prompt, express it through per-shot `### 注意力控制`, focus, light, motion, gaze, sound, composition, and landing frames.

For the whole segment, decide:

- first attention target
- second attention target
- hidden or delayed information
- reveal moment
- emotional, product, or narrative landing point
- what the viewer should not notice yet

For every shot, define:

- primary attention target
- secondary attention target
- attention guide: light, movement, gaze, line, color, sound, foreground, focus, scale, or negative space
- reveal or withholding strategy
- where the eye should rest at the end of the shot

## Attention Tools

Use these tools deliberately:

- light contrast: brightest or most shaped light carries priority
- motion contrast: the moving element draws the eye; stillness can dominate if everything else moves
- gaze line: characters can direct attention without explaining
- foreground obstruction: hides, delays, or frames the subject
- focus plane: sharpness decides importance
- scale: size in frame sets weight
- sound cue: prepares attention before the visual reveal
- entry timing: a subject entering after the frame is established gets priority
- negative space: empty area creates anticipation or emotional pressure
- color isolation: one color can become the attention anchor

## Attention Order

Avoid shots where everything competes equally.

Use a clean order:

```text
first look -> guided shift -> reveal/change -> landing point
```

Examples:

- first look: cold bathroom mirror fog
- guided shift: finger wipes a narrow clear path
- reveal/change: perfume bottle edge appears inside the cleared strip
- landing point: product highlight stops in the clean reflection

## What Not To Show

Director judgment includes withholding.

Do not show everything immediately if delayed attention creates stronger impact. Hide through:

- back view
- edge of frame
- shadow
- reflection
- partial prop
- off-screen sound
- foreground body or object
- shallow depth

Only withhold information when the reveal is clear and generatable.

## Shot Distance

Choose distance by attention purpose:

- wide shot: make the viewer understand space, power relationship, solitude, scale, or movement path
- medium shot: make the viewer read body choice, hand action, product use, or relationship tension
- close-up: make the viewer read face, breath, eye movement, mouth, jaw, or emotional leakage
- insert: make the viewer read material, grip, texture, product detail, or a decisive object change

Do not use a wide shot for emotion that needs face or hands. Do not use a close-up when the important information is spatial relationship.

## Failure Modes

Revise if:

- the shot has many beautiful details but no priority
- the camera moves without changing attention
- the product, face, or action is visible but not dominant at the right moment
- the viewer is asked to understand emotion through adjectives instead of where the eye is guided
- the final frame does not leave a clear attention landing point
